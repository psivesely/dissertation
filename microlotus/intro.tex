Nearly $30$ years after the introduction of the first lattice-based cryptosystem~\cite{STOC:Ajtai96}, lattice-based cryptography is now in widespread deployment. Following NIST standardization of the ML-KEM key exchange mechanism and the ML-DSA and Falcon signatures, lattice-based primitives have been integrated into applications used by billions: TLS, SSH, Signal and iMessage, Zoom, WireGuard and IKEv2, and MLS, among others. Lattice-based fully homomorphic encryption sees increasing real-world deployment as well.

As lattice-based cryptography and succinct arguments have both matured, interest in \emph{proving statements about lattice-based cryptography} has grown in tandem. One important use case is building efficient versions of more complex primitives (aggregate, blind, group, multi, threshold, and ring signatures) by verifying simpler lattice-based signatures inside a SNARK~\cite{PQCRYPTO:BCOS20,AC:LyuNgu22,C:AABKT24}. The algebraic work to verify such schemes consists of ring operations, most often over power-of-two cyclotomic rings $\ring_p = \integers_p[X]/(X^n + 1)$ with $n$ a power of two. We restrict our attention to odd primes $p$ that fully split the cyclotomic, i.e., $p \equiv 1 \pmod{2n}$, so that $\ring_p \cong \field_p^n$ and ring multiplication reduces to coordinatewise multiplication via the Chinese remainder transform (CRT). This is the setting that admits the fastest FFT-style CRT algorithms, and it covers the rings used by the vast majority of deployed lattice-based schemes.

We put forward a new framework for efficiently proving cyclotomic ring arithmetic via a polynomial interactive oracle reduction (PIOR) set over (field extensions of) the base field $\field_p$ that takes advantage of the CRT decomposition to perform slot-wise multiplication. We also introduce and implement a polynomial commitment scheme (PCS) \emph{Microlotus} that supports evaluation of base field polynomials at extension field points with zero embedding overhead for all odd primes, built using Basefold, random foldable codes, and an odd prime field tower construction to support ring-switch packing.

Working over $\field_p$ has several advantages. First, it allows us to eliminate or reduce the dependence of the prover, verifier, and proof size on $n$ compared to working over $\ring_p$ directly. Second, by working over extension fields $\field_{p^k}$ we can use standard randomized reduction techniques without the repetition necessitated by the small exceptional set of $\ring_p$~\cite{C:AlbLai21}. Third, the state of proof machinery---from interactive oracle reductions to lookup tables to bounds on mutual correlated agreement---is by far more developed for fields than any other algebraic setting.

\parhead{A CRT-domain PIOR for ring arithmetic}
Our PIOR proves statements of the form $As = t$ over fully splitting cyclotomic rings. Our starting observation is that since $\ring_p \cong \field_p^n$, we can fold the CRT matrix $\tilde{C}$ directly into the virtual polynomial of a \emph{single} sumcheck expressing the full matrix-vector product, rather than composing separate transform sub-protocols. The CRT-domain virtual polynomials $A_C$, $s_C$, $t_C$ are never materialized as committed oracles: they are defined implicitly from the coefficient-domain oracles $\tilde{A}$, $\tilde{s}$, $\tilde{t}$ and the CRT-matrix multilinear extension $\tilde{C}$, and a second sumcheck resolves the CRT-domain claims back to evaluation claims on the committed coefficient-domain oracles. We show that the CRT evaluation tables needed for the first sumcheck can be built in $O(Mn\log n)$ time, and that the verifier can evaluate $\tilde{C}$ directly in time $O(n)$.
% We note that, concretely, the verifier time will be dominated by verifying the PCS evaluation, and not a single computation of $\tilde{C}$; while asymptotically this places our PIOR verifier time behind some $O(\log nNM)$ protocols, this was borne of a conscious design decision to prioritize concrete SNARK performance.
This yields the following asymptotic efficiency in terms of extension field elements/operations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Prover:} $O(NMn)$
    \item \emph{Verifier:} $O(n + \log NM)$, plus $3$ oracle queries
    \item \emph{Proof size:} $O(\log Mn)$
\end{itemize}
A holographic specialization for public $A$ eliminates the second sumcheck entirely, reducing the proof size to $2\log(Mn)$ extension field elements and the output to a single evaluation claim on each of the committed witness oracle and the indexed oracle. This holographic case corresponds to how our PIOR would be used in a preprocessing SNARK.

Compared to the prior state of the art, this protocol removes $n$ from the proof size (versus ring-native approaches), removes a factor of $\log n$ from the prover (versus NTT-circuit unrolling), and reduces the ring dimension's contribution to the verifier from multiplicative to additive. The resulting $O(n)$ additive verifier term arises from two evaluations of $\tilde{C}$ (\cref{lem:m-eval}), which we show can each be computed in $O(n)$ field operations by exploiting the periodicity of the CRT roots. Before arriving at this CRT-domain protocol, we developed a coefficient-domain sumcheck that proves ring multiplication directly as a negacyclic circulant matrix--vector product, bypassing the CRT decomposition entirely and recovering an $O(\log n)$ verifier. However, the coefficient-domain convolution entangles the column-index and coefficient-index variables, producing folding challenges that fix the oracle evaluation points at distinct last-$\nu$ coordinates. This precludes the shared-coordinate PCS consolidation that our CRT-domain protocol enables. In our ring Plonkish SpeedySpartan example (\cref{sec:reducing-commitments}), these PCS optimizations---including selector-polynomial merging, free multi-point reduction, ring-switch packing, and interleaved BaseFold opening---save the verifier significantly more time than the single $O(n)$ CRT evaluation costs, given that $n$ typically ranges from $64$ to $512$ at the $128$-bit security level. We made the practical choice to prioritize this concrete efficiency over asymptotics.

We highlight how our approach improves on past techniques to prove ring arithmetic over fields and Galois rings in~\cref{sec:ml-related-work}.

\parhead{The Microlotus PCS}
We implement \textsc{Microlotus}, a multilinear polynomial commitment scheme that combines the Basefold IOPP with random foldable linear codes, our odd-prime tower construction, ring-switch packing~\cite{EC:DiaPos25}, and oracle skipping. \textsc{Microlotus} supports evaluating $v$-variate polynomials $f \in \field_p^{\mathsf{ml}}[\bm{X}]$ at points $\bm{x} \in \extensionfield^v$ for any extension $\extensionfield/\field_p$ with $\operatorname{char}(\field_p) > 2$ and $[\extensionfield : \field_p]$ a power of two. Because it uses random foldable codes as the underlying error-correcting code, \textsc{Microlotus} avoids the structured-subgroup requirements of Reed--Solomon-based schemes such as FRI and WHIR, and thus works for \emph{all} odd primes $p$ without restriction on the multiplicative structure of $p^k - 1$.

We benchmark \textsc{Microlotus} over a degree-$16$ tower extension of $\field_{257}$ (the natural setting for SWIFFT) and compare against Basefold with Reed--Solomon codes over degree-$3$ Goldilocks, WHIR over degree-$3$ Goldilocks, and Brakedown over a $255$-bit prime. The \textsc{Microlotus} prover is over $10\times$ faster than WHIR and Basefold and comparable to Brakedown for polynomials of practical size, while generating proofs roughly $2\times$ the size of WHIR and Basefold but far smaller than Brakedown's $O(\sqrt{n})$. Verification is over $10\times$ faster than Brakedown and $2\times$ faster than Basefold for larger polynomials, while remaining about $6\times$ slower than WHIR. 

These benchmarks should be viewed with the caveats that the right choice of arithmetization domain generally has significantly more impact on end-to-end performance than PCS-level efficiency differences, and that our scheme may be instantiated with any alternative PCS that supports the appropriate field.

\parhead{Contributions}
In summary, this work makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{A PIOR for cyclotomic ring multiplication} (\cref{sec:matrix-vector-pior}). We present a sumcheck-based PIOR for proving $As = t$ over fully splitting cyclotomic rings with the above-stated asymptotics. A holographic variant for public $A$ eliminates the second sumcheck, reducing proof size to $2\log(Mn)$ extension field elements, with a single evaluation claim on each of the committed witness oracle and the indexed oracle. We prove round-by-round knowledge soundness, allowing easy composition with other protocols and secure Fiat--Shamir compilation in the quantum random oracle model.
    \item \textbf{An odd-prime tower construction} (\cref{sec:odd-prime-tower}). We introduce a tower of quadratic extensions over any odd prime $p$, with efficient multiplication, squaring, and inversion algorithms. Multiplication in $\field_{p^k}$ can be implemented with $k^{\log 3}$ $\field_p$-multiplications.
    \item \textbf{\textsc{Microlotus}: a multilinear PCS implementation} (\cref{sec:microlotus}). We implement a PCS combining Basefold, random linear codes, our odd-prime tower, ring-switch packing, and oracle skipping. Over a degree-$16$ tower of $\field_{257}$, the prover is over $10\times$ faster than WHIR and Basefold for polynomials of practical size, and has similar performance to Brakedown with asymptotically smaller proofs.
    \item \textbf{A ring Plonkish SpeedySpartan} (\cref{sec:reducing-commitments}). We sketch how to construct a general SNARK for ring arithmetic using our PIOR. We then apply a series of optimizations that illustrate how our CRT-domain protocol design enables zero-cost oracle merging and query claim reduction, and highlight compatibility with ring-switch packing and additional PCS-level optimizations. In all, five claims on five oracles are reduced to two Basefold openings with shared folding and Merkle trees.
\end{enumerate}

\parhead{Further highlights and discussion}
Here we highlight a few additional merits of our framework. Our PIOR outputs evaluation claims for polynomials over $\field_p$ at points in $\field_{p^k}$, where $p$ is an odd prime. Using random foldable codes, Microlotus gives an efficient PCS for all such $p$, but some schemes defined only for specific $p$ can provide faster verification or smaller proofs:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item When $p^k-1$ has high $2$-adicity, (constrained) Reed--Solomon codes can be used with WHIR~\cite{EPRINT:ACFY24}, yielding the best concrete verification times. 
    \item PCSs for cyclotomic rings using the same base field modulus, via the reduction in~\cite{hachi}, including lattice-based commitments, can offer the best concrete proof sizes~\cite{serval, hachi}.
\end{itemize}
We note when using Reed--Solomon codes that to support polynomials of $v$ variables, $2^v$ must divide $p^k-1$. Even factoring in packing, the base fields of lattice-based schemes like Kyber, Falcon, LB-VRF, and CKKS thus support only $16,18,19$, and $21$ variables (respectively, at the first power-of-two $k$ such that $p^k\geq 2^{100}$), constraining circuit size.

On the other hand, BabyBear, Mersenne31, and Goldilocks, which are home to some of the most advanced production SNARKs, all support more than $30$ variables. The reader may at this point be questioning the relevance of these fields, given the absence of lattice-based schemes defined on them. After all, we have emphasized that the choice of base field should be driven primarily by the computation being arithmetized: the arithmetization overhead from a mismatched base field will almost always dominate the efficiency difference between polynomial commitment schemes. We explain as follows.

\parhead{Beyond verifying lattice cryptography: SWIFFT as an in-circuit hash}
We highlight one potential use of our PIOR beyond verifying lattice cryptography: enabling use of parameter-scaled SWIFFT~\cite{FSE:LMPR08} as an in-circuit hash. The (generalized) SWIFFT hash $t$ of a message $s$ is exactly the computation $As_b=t$ over $R_p$, where $s_b$ is the (centered) base-$b$ representation. The original parameters are $n=64$, $p=257$, and $b=2$, but for larger fields it is possible to safely increase $b$.

This approach is particularly attractive in light of ongoing cryptanalytic concerns regarding algebraic hash functions optimized for proof systems: recent Gröbner-basis analyses~\cite{EPRINT:GraKosRec25,ToSC:BBLP22} have identified inaccuracies in Poseidon's original security model that lead to misestimated round requirements, while SWIFFT's security rests on the better-studied worst-case hardness of ideal lattice problems. Moreover, SWIFFT is one of the fastest hash functions both in-circuit (when the field matches the SWIFFT modulus) and out-of-circuit, where NTT-based evaluation is competitive with SHA-256 throughput.

As an example parameterization for $31$-bit fields BabyBear and Mersenne31, setting $b=2^{16}$ and $n=512$ means only decomposing into two elements before hashing, where correctness of decomposition may be efficiently verified via range proofs and/or lookups. These parameters yield an estimated $136$ bits of collision resistance\footnote{Per \url{https://github.com/malb/lattice-estimator}.}. Using SWIFFT to compress a long transcript and applying a single iteration of a symmetric compression function (e.g., BLAKE3) to the (short) SWIFFT output incurs little overhead, while yielding a hash function suitable to instantiate a random oracle~\cite{FC:VGSGJK22}.

Systems like SWIRL~\cite{swirl}, which use LogUp-based buses to link heterogeneous sub-circuits of different sizes within a single proof, may enable further optimizations. In particular, if one picks the SWIFFT modulus to be $p^t$ for sufficiently large $t$ and $n$ that RSIS is secure for norm bound $\beta = p$, then every $\field_p$-element fed to SWIFFT already satisfies the norm constraint without any digit decomposition. The LogUp buses could then handle the interaction between $\field_p$-arithmetic in the main circuit and $\field_{p^t}$-arithmetic in the SWIFFT sub-circuit within a unified proof.
